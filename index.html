What is a Lesson Study?

A lesson study is a collaborative approach to improving learning and instruction by exploring, applying, and reflecting on a lesson sequence. Lesson studies are designed around a cycle of instruction that builds understanding, flexibility, and responsiveness to learners.

The overarching goals of Lesson Study are to build pathways that enable continual growth of the knowledge, interpersonal resources, and motivation required to improve teaching and learning. Teachers‚Äô agency is honored, and their efficacy increases as they design and witness ever-improving cycles of instruction.

Chapter 2: Preparing to Teach

‚Äã

Let's get ready to teach this lesson sequence!

Choose a Text Set

The Ranking Evidence lesson sequence features five text set options. The first step in preparing to teach the lesson sequence is to select the text set you will use with your students.

Consider the following questions as a guide for your decision:

  • Which topics will be most interesting to your students?

  • Which text set provides opportunities that best meet the needs of your learners?

  • Which text sets are more accessible to your learners?

To get started, take some time to explore the full text set in each option below. You don't necessarily need to do a close reading of every single text in each text set option at this point, but it would be helpful to open and preview each text to get a sense of the format, length, vocabulary, level of complexity, etc.

One other note to consider as you are choosing a topic. Sometimes teachers feel like they need to avoid selecting text set topics that their students are unfamiliar with. However, in NWP's experience with text sets in the College, Career, and Community Writer's Program (C3WP), teachers often found that the text set topics their students didn't know much (or anything about) provided the highest engagement. So, keep that in mind when you are thinking about which topic you might choose.

Close Reading of Lesson Sequence

In preparation for teaching, take some time to do a close reading of the Ranking Evidence Lesson Sequence for the text set you selected.

As you read the lesson sequence, make note of

  • Number of suggested days and overall pacing

  • Resources and handouts to prepare

  • Student skills/conceptual understandings

  • Connections to your curriculum and/or assessment standards

  • Important or tricky teacher moves

  • Experiences/learning activities that your students will enjoy and find motivating

  • Anything that seems like it might be challenging for your students

Reflect and Share

Now that you have taken some time to choose a text set and to look closely at the lesson sequence, go to the Preparing to Teach Discussion to share what you discovered.

Reflect on the following:

  • Which text set did you choose and why?

  • What adaptations or adjustments do you think you might need to make?

  • Other questions or ideas

Remember, please also reply to each other offering appreciation, asking questions, and adding new ideas that spark discussion.

Looking Ahead: Preparation for Lesson Three

Lesson Three: Jigsaw

Depending on grade-level and students‚Äô experience with reading, annotating, and discussion, this lesson may take 2 class periods. Be sure to plan accordingly and don‚Äôt rush students too much. Having said that, accountability to their peers is a central part of making a jigsaw work, so also don‚Äôt drag it on too long. There should be some sense of urgency in completing the activities to keep students moving forward.

It really helps streamline the jigsaw process to copy each of the texts in a different color. That way students can find like groups and mixed groups really easily. To prepare for a jigsaw, teachers typically divide the total number of students in the class by the total number of articles and make a couple additional copies of each one. For example, if you have a class of 35 students and 5 articles, you would make 9-10 copies of each.

There are a few strategies you might use for distributing the texts. Again, color-coding makes each of these approaches easier. :

  • Set the piles of articles out on a table and allow students to choose which one they want to read

  • If students are sitting in partners or groups, hand out the articles by group

  • If you have varied reading levels and want to differentiate, you can distribute articles based on that. The articles represent a range of lengths and levels of difficulty so a teacher can take this approach.

  • Be sure not to skip the step where students get to check in with someone else who read the same source as them. This is key to making sure everyone is successful in the jigsaw. Be sure to do this before you hand out the jigsaw note-catcher so they actually talk about the source rather than just copying each other.
